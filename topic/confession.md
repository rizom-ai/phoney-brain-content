A central focus is confession as a social and moral pressure point: private shame turned public, where one admission forces everyone else into disclosure. What looks like honesty quickly becomes a test of control, as each person tries to manage how their wrongdoing will be received. The scene is less about catharsis than exposure, comparison, and self-justification. Confession here is not a simple route to redemption or absolution; it destabilizes the room, strips away moral self-image, and reveals how truth-telling is often tense, risky, and incomplete rather than cleansing.
