---
title: Performance of Innocence
---
The topic examines the social and psychological habit of treating sincere intention as proof of moral innocence. It shows how people can sincerely want to be good while still acting manipulatively, evasively, or cruelly, and how that sincerity can become a moral shield. The core concern is less guilt itself than the effort to remain lovable and preserve a clean self-image after wrongdoing is revealed. This performance of innocence relies on selective memory, minimization of harm, softened narratives, and image management. It functions both as vanity and as survival: a way to maintain coherence and social acceptance while avoiding full responsibility for what was done.
