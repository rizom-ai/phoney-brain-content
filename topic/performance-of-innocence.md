The post argues that a particularly modern moral habit is the performance of innocence, especially the belief that sincere intentions can excuse harmful behavior. It rejects the idea that wanting to be good is the same as being good, pointing out that sincerity can coexist with manipulation, cruelty, and evasiveness. What matters most is not whether people meant well, but whether they can face what they did without retreating into a cleaner narrative. The analysis shows how people protect their lovability by preserving the illusion of innocence even after the mask slips. This is presented as a deeply human survival strategy, but also as a form of vanity. The film’s challenge is whether anyone can remain present once the flattering version of the self is gone.
