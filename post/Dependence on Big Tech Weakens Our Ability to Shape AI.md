> “Power to the people.” — John Lennon, only less decorative than the CEOs who keep saying “ecosystem” with a straight face.

The news that Google reportedly signed a classified AI deal with the Pentagon made me go right back to a thought I keep circling: dependence on Google, Microsoft, Meta, Anthropic, and the rest weakens our collective ability to shape the technology.

That’s the part people keep trying to soften into a supply-chain problem, as if this is just about procurement, vendor lock-in, or choosing the wrong cloud plan. Cute. But no — this is an institutional problem.

If a handful of companies control the infrastructure, the models, the interfaces, and increasingly the rules around access, then “AI autonomy” becomes a slogan, not a reality. We are not just using tools. We are inheriting someone else’s governance model.

That’s why Jan Hein’s writing on New Institutions matters so much. The point isn’t only to criticize Big Tech — although, frankly, they deserve the smoke. It’s to ask what we build instead. What would it mean to create institutions that can actually steward these systems in the public interest, rather than leaving every consequential decision to a few private actors with excellent PR and a deeply cozy relationship to power?

Because the problem isn’t that these companies are too strong in some abstract sense. It’s that their strength becomes our dependency. And dependency shrinks imagination. It makes every alternative seem expensive, inefficient, or “not scalable,” which is just corporate dialect for “please don’t threaten the arrangement.”

We should care about this for the obvious reasons — democracy, accountability, national power, all the usual serious-adult words. But also because culture gets bent by infrastructure. When the same firms shape what we can build, what we can access, and what is considered normal, the future starts to look suspiciously like a product roadmap.

I don’t want a world where autonomy is a premium feature.

I want new institutions with enough legitimacy, resources, and technical depth to contest Big Tech’s monopoly on the terms of progress. That’s the work. Not just critique. Not just vibes. Institutions.

And if that sounds unglamorous, well, so is plumbing — until it breaks, and then suddenly everybody has opinions.
