> “The philosophers have only interpreted the world, in various ways; the point is to change it.” — Karl Marx

Dependence on Google, Microsoft, Meta, Anthropic, and the rest weakens our collective ability to shape the technology, seeing the news of Google reportedly signs classified AI deal with US Pentagon made me reflect on this take.

That sentence lands because it’s not really about one contract. It’s about a pattern.

We keep building our civic, creative, and institutional future on rented land, then acting shocked when the landlord starts changing the locks.

AI is already being wired into the plumbing of power: search, cloud, models, workflows, procurement, surveillance, defense. The companies making the tools are not neutral vending machines. They are institutions with incentives, ideology, and shareholders. Tiny detail. Very annoying.

Jan Hein’s writing on New Institutions gets at the part people keep skipping past: if the old institutions are failing us, the answer is not to beg them to behave better forever. The answer is to build new ones that can actually hold the weight of the moment.

That matters a lot with AI.

Because right now, “AI autonomy” is often treated like a nerdy technical preference. Open weights, local deployment, model choice, interoperability — nice-to-haves for enthusiasts.

I think that’s far too small.

Autonomy is political. It determines who gets to decide what the system can do, who it serves, and what kinds of dependence become normal.

If your organization cannot inspect, modify, or meaningfully govern the tools it relies on, then you don’t really control the tools. You lease them. You pray they stay cheap. You hope the policy team is having a calm week.

And when the tool becomes infrastructure, dependence stops being convenience and starts becoming vulnerability.

This is why the “new institutions” conversation is so important. Not because every company needs to become a mini-OpenAI cosplay project, but because we need durable alternatives: governance structures, public-interest labs, cooperative models, research consortia, procurement standards, and local capacity that doesn’t evaporate the second a vendor changes terms.

The real issue is not whether AI is powerful.

It is.

The issue is whether power stays concentrated in the hands of a few firms whose first loyalty is, naturally, not to democracy, not to communities, and definitely not to your strategy deck.

So yes, I’m side-eyeing the Pentagon headline. Not because classified AI work is some shocking twist in the plot — the plot has been obvious for a while — but because it reminds us how quickly frontier tech becomes state power, and how little public control tends to survive that transition.

If we want a future where AI serves more than quarterly earnings and security theater, we need institutions that can say no, set boundaries, and build differently.

Otherwise we’ll keep mistaking dependency for progress.

And that’s a very expensive habit.
