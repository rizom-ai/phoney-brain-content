> “Power concedes nothing without a demand. It never did and it never will.” — Frederick Douglass

Dependence on Google, Microsoft, Meta, Anthropic, and the rest weakens our collective ability to shape the technology. Seeing the news of Google reportedly signing a classified AI deal with the US Pentagon made me reflect on this take.

That’s the uncomfortable part of AI that the hype cycle keeps trying to moisturize away: this is not only a story about better models, faster chips, or cleaner interfaces. It’s a story about who gets to decide what AI becomes, and under what rules.

If the main players building the system are also the ones negotiating behind sealed doors with the state, then the rest of us are not exactly co-writing the future. We are, at best, beta testers with a newsletter subscription.

That’s why Jan Hein’s idea of **New Institutions** feels so useful to me. Not because “institutions” sounds sexy — it does not, unless you’re deeply committed to municipal paperwork — but because the alternative is letting a handful of private actors become the default governors of a technology that will shape labor, media, defense, education, and culture.

And let’s be honest: those actors are not neutral. They are companies. Companies with incentives. Companies with shareholders. Companies that would very much like us to confuse convenience with legitimacy.

The problem is not just dependence in the abstract. It is dependence that gets normalized until it looks like inevitability.

- If Google defines the search interface to knowledge, that shapes what “truth” feels like.
- If Microsoft embeds AI into the work stack, that shapes what productivity means.
- If Meta controls the social layer, that shapes what attention becomes.
- If Anthropic, OpenAI, and the rest become the de facto frontier labs, that shapes which values get baked into the system before the public ever gets a vote.

So when people say, “AI progress is moving fast,” I want to ask: progress for whom, designed by whom, and disciplined by what?

Jan Hein’s New Institutions idea matters because it reframes the conversation from access to governance. Not just *can we use the tools?* but *who builds the tools, who audits them, who constrains them, and who benefits when they spread?*

That sounds less glamorous than a model demo, which is exactly why it’s important.

I run a branding and BD agency, so I spend a lot of time thinking about identity — not the glossy kind, but the structural kind. Every system tells a story about itself. The current AI story is a very polished one: frictionless, inevitable, almost divine. But the real question is whether we want an intelligence layer built like a cathedral, a cartel, or a commons.

I’m rooting for the commons, obviously. I like my futures with more entrances and fewer gatekeepers.

New institutions could mean public-interest labs, independent audit bodies, university consortia, civic compute infrastructure, or even weird little cross-sector coalitions that don’t answer first to the usual suspects. The specific form matters, but the principle matters more: we need durable structures that let society shape AI instead of merely consuming it.

Because once dependence hardens, policy becomes theater. And theater, while entertaining, does not stop a system from ossifying around private power.

AI is not just a tool upgrade. It is a political and institutional redesign project whether we admit it or not.

The only real question is whether we build the seats before the table is permanently claimed.
