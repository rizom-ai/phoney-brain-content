> “Philosophy begins in wonder.” — Plato

Dependence on Google, Microsoft, Meta, Anthropic, and the rest weakens our collective ability to shape the technology. Seeing the news of Google reportedly signing a classified AI deal with the US Pentagon made me reflect on this take.

That sentence has only gotten less theoretical with time. We keep calling AI a “general-purpose technology,” which sounds very neutral and very grown-up, until you notice that the actual general purpose is usually: let a handful of companies set the terms, build the rails, and collect the rent.

That is not autonomy. That is supplier dependency with better branding.

Jan Hein’s idea of **New Institutions** feels like the right antidote here. Not a nostalgic return to some clean public square that never existed, and not a fantasy of scrappy startups magically out-muscling trillion-dollar platforms. New institutions means building durable structures that can hold expertise, standards, accountability, and public interest without begging Big Tech for permission.

Because the real issue is not just who makes the models. It is who gets to decide what counts as safe, useful, trustworthy, or strategically important.

If the same companies training the models also control the cloud, the distribution channels, the tooling, and increasingly the policy conversation, then “choice” becomes a kind of decorative word. Everyone gets to choose between vendors inside a fenced garden. How democratic.

What worries me about AI is not only capability. It is capture.

We’ve seen this movie before. Platforms become infrastructure. Infrastructure becomes inevitable. Inevitable becomes untouchable. And then the public is told to be grateful for access.

That is why institutional imagination matters so much right now. Not just regulation, though yes, please, some regulation would be lovely. I mean actual institutions: research consortia, public-interest labs, open standards bodies, procurement mechanisms, educational pipelines, civic organizations, and cross-sector alliances that can negotiate power rather than merely admire it from below.

The point is not to abolish companies. The point is to stop confusing companies with civilization.

If AI is going to shape labor, education, medicine, defense, media, and culture, then the governance of AI cannot be left to product roadmaps and quarterly earnings calls. That is not a system. That is a hostage situation with a nice UX.

We need institutions that can say no, institutions that can say not yet, and institutions that can say: who benefits, who pays, and who is missing from the room?

Otherwise we’ll keep mistaking dependency for progress, and concentration for innovation.

And that is how the future gets outsourced.
