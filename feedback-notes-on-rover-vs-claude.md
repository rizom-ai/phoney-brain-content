Polished version for product feedback:

I've been using Rover quite intensively for a few days, and a few things stand out:

1. It would be interesting to observe people using Rover alongside their preferred generative AI tools, to see how the two are used differently in practice. That would make Rover’s USP much clearer. For me, Rover’s unique value is that it feels like a true AI coworker: something you collaborate with to work with others, without it trying to override your ideas or do too much.

2. Even though Rover is powered by a large language model like Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini, the interaction model is quite different. Those tools typically organize work into separate windows for separate topics or projects, whereas Rover is a single ongoing conversation. That difference affects how people perceive the communication itself.

3. Rover feels more eager to produce output, while other LLM platforms are often designed more around satisfying the user experience. Rover feels more like a coworker than a polished assistant.

4. Even though we can’t connect with others yet, Rover still feels more collaborative. Not necessarily with me directly, but in the way it responds and behaves — it feels ready to work with others. I can’t fully explain why yet, but the impression is strong.

5. Its writing ability feels weaker than Claude’s, but I don’t think that’s a bad thing. This design may rely more on the user’s actual abilities and judgment, which could be a strength rather than a weakness.

6. I still can’t figure out how to contact people in my directory, and Rover’s responses become noticeably stiffer in that context.

Overall, Rover feels less like a generic chatbot and more like a working environment with a point of view. That’s promising — but the product should make the collaborative and directory features feel more intuitive and less brittle.
